How fen gets built for one practice

Not a feature backlog. A handful of real sessions, spent understanding one Tuesday at a time.

Before any screen gets designed, the work is understanding what a real day actually looks like — where the gaps are, and where something quietly slips through.

Session one

Sit through the actual gaps between sessions, the inbox pile, the evening notes catch-up — not a hypothetical Tuesday.

Session two

Map what's invisible

Find where relationships quietly slip — which clients, which referrers, which moments get missed.

Session three

Prototype together

Build the exact screen that would have caught last month's silent drop-off, tested against real history.

Session four

Refine, then hand off

Adjust based on what actually gets used in week one, not what looked good in the pitch.

Not CRM CRM

A pipeline was built for deals. This was built for people who come back — or should have.

Traditional CRMs assume a relationship ends at a sale. Practice-management EHRs assume a relationship is just a series of appointments. Neither one notices the space in between, where trust is either kept warm or quietly lost.

Sales CRM

  • Deals, stages, quotas

  • Built for acquisition

Fen

  • Cadence, continuity, follow-through
  • Relationship is the whole point
  • Built for retention and trust

Two nodes, one system

Every relationship in the practice reduces to one of two shapes.

Node one — the client

Drifts away slowly

Rarely a cancellation, never an announcement — the gap between sessions just quietly widens until they're gone. Fen watches cadence, not just the calendar, and flags the drift while there's still time to reach out (Bugatti et al., 2024).

Node two — the referring doctor

Builds through natural contact

Referral relationships don't grow on their own — they grow on being acknowledged. Fen tracks who sent what, when they last heard back, and nudges the loop closed before goodwill quietly runs dry.
